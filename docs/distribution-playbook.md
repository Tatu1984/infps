Do these in order. The first three are ~30 minutes total and matter most.

   - Sidebar → **Sitemaps** → enter `sitemap.xml` → Submit (if not already done).

     - `/resources/soc-2-readiness-checklist`
3. **Confirm GA4 is tracking.** GA4 → Reports → Realtime → open your site in another tab → you should see yourself.

### 🟠 This month
4. **Set up Google Business Profiles** for London + New York at [business.google.com](https://business.google.com). Free, high-intent local leads. Use the same NAP (name/address/phone) as the site. Add services, photos, and a link to the matching `/locations/...` page.

### 🟢 Ongoing
8. Each time a new article ships, request indexing for its URL (step 2) and post it to LinkedIn (Part 2).
9. Once a month, check GA4 → Reports → Acquisition to see which channels bring visitors, and Clarity (if enabled) to watch where people drop off.

## Part 2 — Ready-to-post LinkedIn content

Post from your **personal** profile (founder-led posts outperform company-page posts ~5–10x in reach). End each with a link to the article. Reply to every comment in the first 2 hours — it drives reach.

### Post 1 — Cost of an in-house team
> Most CTOs budget the salary and forget the other half of the bill.
>
> A 6-engineer team in New York isn't ~$1.5M. Fully loaded — payroll taxes, benefits, equipment, recruiting fees, and the 2–4 months each hire spends ramping — it's closer to $1.9M–2.3M in year one. London: ~£1.1M–1.4M.
>
> And that assumes you can *fill* all six seats. Most teams take 6–11 months to hire six senior engineers. So you're often 9–15 months and $1.5M+ in before the team ships at the velocity you planned.
>
> Sometimes that's the right spend — if engineering is your moat, hire. But if the bottleneck is shipping a roadmap this year, the math often points elsewhere.
>
> Broke the full cost breakdown (US vs UK) down here 👇
> [link to /insights/cost-of-in-house-engineering-team-us-uk]

### Post 2 — Build vs Buy
> "Should we build it or buy it?" is the wrong question in 2026.
>
> It's a three-way split: build, buy, or partner — and the answer is different for each part of your stack on the same day.
>
> The test that settles most of it: is this a *differentiator* (why customers pick you) or *table stakes* (what they expect but never praise)?
>
> Build your differentiators. Buy your table stakes. The expensive mistakes are almost always the inversion — custom-building billing, or buying a generic platform for the workflow that's actually your moat.
>
> Full framework + scoring rubric here 👇
> [link to /insights/build-vs-buy-software-2026]

### Post 3 — SOC 2 (pair with the lead magnet)
> You don't need to buy a compliance platform to get SOC 2.
>
> We've taken growth-stage SaaS companies from zero controls to a SOC 2 Type I in 90 days by treating controls as code, not policies. The platform is useful in year two when evidence volume explodes — premature in year one.
>
> The part teams underestimate isn't the policies. It's evidence collection: if a control can't be triggered by a cron job or a CI step, it'll fail in year two.
>
> I put the exact 30-point checklist our engineers use into a free download 👇
> [link to /resources/soc-2-readiness-checklist]

### Post 4 — Hiring an AI partner
> Half the "AI consultancies" pitching you rebranded 18 months ago. Some ship production systems. Many ship a slide deck and a thin wrapper around an API.
>
> Three questions that separate the builders from the storytellers:
> 1. How will you measure whether this is good enough to ship — and who decides?
> 2. What happens when the model is confidently wrong? Show me the fallback.
> 3. What's the per-request cost and latency at OUR real volume, not the demo's?
>
> If they lead with "we use the latest frontier model" instead of your problem, that's your answer.
>
> Full buyer's guide + RFP questions 👇
> [link to /insights/how-to-hire-ai-consulting-partner]

### Post 5 — Fractional teams
> Hiring a 20-person engineering team is no longer the default answer for a Series B.
>
> It's the most expensive, slowest, riskiest way to ship a roadmap. A senior fractional pod delivers comparable throughput at 40–60% of fully-loaded in-house cost — no 9-month hiring cycle, no ramp lag, terms that flex on 30–60 day notice.
>
> When is it the WRONG call? If your codebase is your moat and you need engineers who'll be there in 5 years — hire. Fractional shines when the bottleneck is throughput, not retention.
>
> A CTO's guide to the model 👇
> [link to /insights/fractional-engineering-teams-2026]

**Reusable hook formats** for future posts: a contrarian take ("X is the wrong question"), a specific number that surprises ("$1.9M, not $1.5M"), or a short checklist ("3 questions that…"). Always end with one link and one idea — never two.

## Part 3 — Posting cadence (sustainable)

- **2 LinkedIn posts/week** from the founder profile (Tue + Thu mornings, US/UK overlap ~9–11am ET tends to work).
- Rotate: 1 post that drives to an article, 1 that's a standalone insight (no link — pure value, more reach).
- **Engage 15 min/day**: comment on 3–5 posts from your ICP (CTOs/VPEs at growth-stage US/UK SaaS). This is where the actual conversations — and DMs — start.
- Repurpose: each article = 2–3 LinkedIn posts (different angle each) + 1 short "carousel" of the key points.

## Part 5 — What to watch (so you know it's working)

- **Weekly:** GA4 Realtime + Acquisition — is traffic growing, and from where?
- **Per post:** LinkedIn impressions + profile views + DMs. DMs/comments from ICP > vanity likes.
- **Monthly:** Search Console → Performance — which queries are you showing up for, and is average position improving? (This is the leading indicator that SEO is starting to work.)
- **The number that matters:** form submissions + calls booked. Everything else is a proxy.

> Reminder on timeline: expect LinkedIn to produce conversations within weeks; expect SEO/content to produce inbound leads in 3–6 months as articles climb. Both compound. Keep publishing + posting on cadence and the two curves cross sometime around month 4–6.
